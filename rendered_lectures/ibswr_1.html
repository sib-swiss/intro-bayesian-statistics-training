Question: is the mean 0?

Answer: no (its actually -0.25). We can see it is negative better with larger sample sizes.

Computing in R

base R

With the t.test() function

# Generate some Gaussian samples with mean -0.25
test_samples <- rnorm(50, mean = -0.25, sd = 1)
  • test if the mean is 0
# Run a t-test with null hypothesis of mean 0
t.test(test_samples)
## 
##  One Sample t-test
## 
## data:  test_samples
## t = -1.3337, df = 49, p-value = 0.1885
## alternative hypothesis: true mean is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  -0.46750867  0.09451225
## sample estimates:
##  mean of x 
## -0.1864982

Two sample tt-test in R

base R

With the t.test() function

# Generate some Gaussian samples with mean -0.25 and control with mean 0
test_samples <- rnorm(50, mean = -0.25, sd = 1)
control_samples <- rnorm(50, mean = 0, sd = 1)
  • test for a difference in means
# Run a two sample t-test with null hypothesis of equal means
t.test(test_samples, control_samples)
## 
##  Welch Two Sample t-test
## 
## data:  test_samples and control_samples
## t = -1.1571, df = 97.633, p-value = 0.2501
## alternative hypothesis: true difference in means is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  -0.6412473  0.1688956
## sample estimates:
##   mean of x   mean of y 
## -0.18649821  0.04967762

Summary

pp-values are a measure of surprise

  • how unusual would the data be under the null?

For normal data the rescaled sample mean

  • follows a Student tt-distribution
  • becomes more normal with larger sample sizes (CLT)

tt-tests can be employed to examine average differences

  • one sample, paired samples and two samples
  • more groups ANOVA

tt-tests assume no outliers and normality

  • can check with EDA and Q-Q plots
